37. Composing with Csound: adeson TN

Magdalena Klapper

Introduction

I started working with Csound two years ago. After some experiences with other musical programmes I had at that time, I remember it as a meeting with the real world of computer music. So I made friends with Csound at once and became fond of it for the liberty it gives me as a composer working with this programme.

"adeson TN" is my first piece composed with Csound. The basic idea of this piece was to process the voice of my very good friend and excellent jazz singer Kinga Studnicka in many various ways. 

Preparations

The realisation of "adeson TN" is mainly based on the processing of recorded and sampled sounds. I prepared three groups of sound material for this piece.

The first group contained recordings of Kinga's voice, which were made in sound proof chamber at the University of Mining and Metallurgy in Cracow. I recorded one by one the letters of the Polish alphabet, each in three versions: whisper, speech and scream.

The second group consisted of about ten sounds of gongs, which differed in length, pitch and tone colour. These sounds make up the background of the piece.

The last group contained sounds registered from medical equipment during an echocardiographical examination of the heart. These sounds were not used directly in the piece, but their purpose was to modify other sounds. Two very important features of these recordings were a very wide spectrum and a stable but not periodical (!) pulse. After filtering the echo-sounds were used as control signals in Csound to form an envelope of single harmonics amplitudes of computer generated sound. What is the most important, it breaks out the sound periodicity.

The basic principle for "adeson TN" was to take human speech as an instrument rather then a source of information. A word (a group of letters) has its musical colour, and it does not matter whether it means something or not from the linguistic point of view. Such an approach allows the group of letters from the title to be the musical theme of the piece. 

Initiation

The first part of the piece is a theme introduction.

The theme was constructed of seven letters (vowels and consonants) ,which were chosen so as jointly to create an interesting structure. It should be stressed that not only the tone colour but also the element of rhythm is of importance here. Vowels and consonants form two alternating groups in the theme. They are always placed opposite one another during the theme exposition, for instance by a different localisation in space. They also accrue separately. The theme envelope usually passes from an undetermined, uncertain situation, which is expressed by using whispered, noisy sounds, towards concretization illustrated as an estimation of pitch (through spoken sounds to screams at the end).

In this part voice processing causing a timbre deformation is rather limited, to let a listener easily recognise the speech. What gives rise to interest in a listener is an untypical and modified amplitude of spoken sounds, which is something new and not natural. In the next part of the piece a direction of timbre processing is much more advanced and leads to a complete change of tone colour and character of voice sounds, as it almost can not be associated with speech. So the purpose of the first part is to show the theme and other musical material and also to prepare a listener for later forthcoming changes.

We should try at the moment to look at how the above mentioned principles of the structure of the piece are realised when using Csound algorithms.

Working on speech processing in the first part of the piece I used most often instruments number 1, 2, 5, 6 and 9. A few of them may seem to be quite similar but they differ in some important details. All of these instruments read sound file using 'soundin' or 'loscil' opcodes.

Instrument 1 simply reads sounds and places them in one particular point in space between the left and right loudspeaker. Simple amplitude modification is also possible. Instrument 2 lets the sound move fluently in space, which gives more extensive possibilities, than in instrument 1. We can observe in this example, in spite of its triteness, how the musical material can be developed step by step in the piece.

Instrument 2 lets us also change the sound pitch in a simple way. The main purpose of this instrument is to create (by discreet pitch transposition) various human voice timbres. We have recordings of one person voice, but in the piece we can compose a crowd of speaking people (men, women or children), or even something like a singing choir, if we wish. This way of changing the pitch causes a change of sound duration, because of how the 'loscil' opcode acts. Usually this is a rather undesirable effect, but in this particular case the pitch change after the transposition is small enough (to not deform sound too much) to let the duration change of the sound remain unnoticed by a listener.

Instrument 5 extends our possibility of sound processing with a more advanced amplitude modification. The amplitude is controlled by a periodical signal, the pattern of which is held in a score table, and may be repeated with a frequency changing in different ways. Also sound frequency in this instrument may be changed fluently, so we can obtain the effect of glissandi. Instrument 6 is almost the same as instrument 5, but it offers a very specific amplitude modification. The amplitude control signal has two segments of linear or exponential shape. Besides the combinations lin-lin and exp-exp, which are not unusual, there are also possible mixed combinations lin-exp and exp-lin in amplitude envelope, which are much more interesting from the musical point of view.

Instrument 9 takes in great part in the development of the element of rhythm in the first part of the piece. This algorithm uses 'comb' opcode, which adds to the sound an iterated echoes. Then the sound (with echo) is filtered by two band-pass filters with different centre frequencies. The band widths are large at the beginning and gradually narrow, which produces an effect of sound division. At first, the echoes are well-ordered and became more and more irregular for further sounds. This irregularity is obtained by overlapping firs two, then three sounds (played of instrument 9) with different echo frequencies.

Now we can sum up how the group of voice sounds is introduced and processed in the first part of the piece. The character of speech is altered from whisper at the very beginning (the first theme exposition) to screams in the first part climax (the echo series are presented at that moment) and then back to whisper, but a little deformed to prepare a listener to the next part of the piece. The voice is processed mainly in the direction of amplitude modifications and building compact structures of many sounds (talking crowds), to let a listener recognise a speech, but also to attract his attention.

In the first part of the piece the sounds which originate from recorded gongs also appear. Their aim was to create a background and musical setting of the theme. Sounds based on gongs are mysterious, even a little sinister and do not resemble anything of the real word. They have a character of very singular, colourful noise. Let us focus on how these sounds were constructed with Csound.

There is no special instrument in the orchestra to produce these gong-sounds. They are clusters, which came into existence by transposition of a sample of gong to different pitches and then putting together the result. So the method is extremely simple, but the musical effect quite fascinating (mainly because of the gong sound properties). The structure of the gong-cluster is shown in the picture below.

Figure 1. The structure of the cluster based on the gong sample. The gong sample is a single line in the right of left side of the picture The beginnings of all the transposed samples (reversed in the left and not reversed in the right side) meet at the middle of the cluster.
  

Because the sound of a gong has quite a wide spectrum, the clusters made of a gong sample has a character of noise, rather then a set of distinguishable pitches. The pitch transposition of gong sample results in the change of its duration. As all the samples meet together at one point in the middle of the sound (like the picture shows), higher frequencies appear later and decay earlier then lower, which causes an effect of the sound approaching and receding. Additionally, components of the cluster differ in the left-right side localisation. So the movement of sound has two dimensions: front-back and left-right (in the second case every partial moves separately). The relation of pitches of the cluster components creates sense of specific harmony, expressed by the colour of noise. Pitch intervals which were employed in cluster construction were minor and major 3rds and major 2nds (the frequency scaling factors 3/4, 4/5, 5/6 were used during sample reading with the 'loscil' opcode). Major triads were used most often. 

Playing with Sound

After acquainting a listener with the musical material in the first part (sounds and tones, but also musical structures), further work on the piece was an excellent and amusing play with sound for me. I indulged myself in far interference into the sound structure and strong deformation of its character, especially the tone colour. I have also in consideration a development and processing of the musical motifs from the first part.

In the second part I used much more letters then the seven in the theme. There are a few directions of speech treatment in this part.

One direction is a continuation of the way from the firs part. Voice is not deformed very much, so it may be recognised easily, however more letters (different tones) are involved now. Letters are arranged in phrases of precisely chosen tone colour and rhythm, so they create a sense of mystery and anxiety. The principle of altering from whisper to scream is abided here as well.

There are compact sound structures also created, based on one letter iterated with the irregular, fast rhythm, different amplitude and space localisation. It makes an effect, which may be compared to a choir of persons, who mimic each other. Such structures (each of them based on other letter) are overlapped, which is a source of a strong tension increase in the piece, and is used to create local and global climaxes.

Before we pass on to another direction of speech treatment let us focus on the instrument 10 from the orchestra file for a while. This instrument is the most important algorithm of speech processing I have designed for this piece. It is based on an additive analysis and resynthesis. Sound files of recorded voice were analysed with usage of "hetro" utility programme, which created for every sound file its accessory file with the results of an additive analysis. The analysis results were used by instrument 10 to resynthesize the sound files with possibility of fluent modification of the amplitude, frequency and speed of contributing partials. What effect is obtained as the result of this process, from the musical point of view? First of all, instrument 10 is able to transpose voice to different pitches without any change of its duration, and on the contrary, the duration may be changed with no pitch transposition (in other words, change of the pitch and change of the length are independent). Secondary, an additive analyse cause an interesting spectrum deformation, especially in case of unvoiced speech sounds, what is connected with speech sounds properties. The analysis parameters selection caused "combing out" from sampled speech sounds 30 to 50 harmonics. Other components and additional murmurs were cut off. It concerns especially unvoiced speech sounds because its non harmonic spectrum was changed to harmonic, which caused a of large deformation of its tone. This deformation may be so extreme, that a listener could not guess that the sound origins from the speech sample.

Instrument 10 was used to create two classes of sounds.

The first class consists of clusters built of vocal sounds transposed to different pitches. On the contrary to the gong-clusters from the first part, components of these clusters have audible pitch and create substantial harmonisation. The most often used intervals are minor and major 3rds, like in the firs part. The pitch of each component is not stable, but revolves near the basic frequency, which brings a significant diversity.

Sounds of the second class appear in the climax of the piece. They combine the two methods mentioned above: the compact sound structures and the additive analysis and resynthesis of voice. Sounds of speech based on simple voice samples reading are gradually (with time) replaced by the same voice samples processed with the 'adsyn' opcode (instrument 10), which gives an effect of growing unreality of the sound. In the climax of the piece this process is employed in different structures (based on letters from the theme) at the same time.

The background of the second part of the piece is very uniform. It is created of samples of gong sounds, which are read and processed with instrument 4.

Instrument 4 reads samples from a score table using 'table' opcode. The index of the table is a control signal, so the sample may be read with fluent change of speed. The shape of the index control signal is also read from a score table with the 'table' opcode, but in this case the interpolating version of this opcode must be used.

The shapes of index signal I prepared to process gong samples, consist of linear segments with different gradients (like in the picture below).

Figure 2. The example of the index control signal. The sample is read from the end and changes the speed of reading three times with the charge of the reading direction.
 
 

What is the interpretation of using the signal like this one? The speed of sound reading within one segment of the index control signal is constant, because of its linear shape. But various gradients of segments define the change of corresponded speeds. Additionally, a rising line means the direction of reading from the beginning to the end, a sloping line - the opposite direction. So gong samples are read piecemeal, forward or backward, with the abrupt change of speed. Because of the characteristic amplitude of a gong sample (short, steep attack and long, slow decay), reading of this sample with a variable direction produces long sounds with temporary amplitude accumulation and subsidence.

There are two levels of gong sounds produced in the same time during all the second part. One of them consists of higher and faster changing sounds, the other one contains of lower sounds, which amplitude changes are slower. Sounds of both levels begin and end independently of themselves, and their amplitude maximums "miss" each other. This process creates the irregular, but uniform background.

Sounds of gong transposed to low frequencies get a very beautiful, "sandish reflection" in tone colour within high frequency range. Thank to this and to the noisy character of gong sounds the background of the second part has an unique tone and creates an impression of large space.

In the second part a new group of sounds also appear. They are based on the samples of echocardiographical examination of the heart. The purpose of involving these samples is to create a sound in a border-line of synthesis and recorded material, it means the sound, which is synthesised by the computer, but some elements of natural sounds are employed to this synthesis. The advantage of such an approach is creation of the sound, which has an unique tone colour (synthesised by the computer algorithm and quite different from real word sounds), but also is devoid of monotony connected with a periodical character of electronic sounds.

I designed two Csound algorithms to obtain this purpose: instrument 7, and its more advanced version - instrument 8.

Instrument 7 generates a series of sine harmonics. Harmonics are added in a loop, so the number of the partials may be large, different for each sound and may change with time (the loop condition is a control variable). Every partial has its individual amplitude modification, which shape is taken from the recorded "echo-heart" sound. "Echo-heart" samples after reading by the 'soundin' opcode are divided into many signals (as many as generated partials) by filtering the source file with the different bands. In other words, the amplitude control signal of partial x is the "echo-heart" signal filtered by the band-pass filter with the central frequency equal to the frequency of partial x. The filter band width is inversely proportional to the number of partials. Thanks to this approach, values of particular amplitudes are adequate to a frequency distribution in the spectrum of the "echo-heart" sound. The "echo-heart" signal has still very irregular amplitude after filtering (noisy character), so it must be smoothing a little before partials modulation (to not deform harmonic spectrum of generated sound very much). Instruments 7 and 8 are examples of amplitude modulation. The signals of filtered "echo-heart" change as well the partials amplitudes as the spectrum of generated sound (so it is already not strictly harmonic). If the amplitude modification signal has a noisy character, a lot of additional frequencies appear in generated sound, and it gets a noisy character too (what is rather unwonted). There are three methods of smoothing possible in instrument 7: based on 'rms', 'downsamp' and 'integ' opcodes.

Instrument 8 limits the number of harmonics to eleven, however their frequencies may move. This possibility of fluent frequency change (different for different partials) is one more step in the harmony envelope. All partials are divided into two groups, and there are two rules (functions) of frequency change defined, one for each group. It creates an interesting effect of fast sound variability. The main advantage of "echo-heart" sounds is their pulse (connected with heart beating). Sounds generated with instrument 7 and 8 adopt this property, so they also have the meaning as a rhythmical element in the piece, apart from an interesting tone and sense of harmony.

The second part leads to the climax of the piece and then to the return of the theme in its basic form, which brings the piece to the end. 

Conclusion

The piece has a classical form ABA'C. Part A (described as the first one) and part B (described as the second one) stand in contrast with each other. This contrast is obtained mainly by enveloping and processing in the part B, the music material and ideas introduced in the part A. Returning of the theme in the piece climax closes the form. But only the very beginning of the firs part is repeated (A'). The piece is closed by the short fragment based on the gong sounds from the second part, which lets the piece to gradually fade away. The major triad appears at the very end to sum up the direction of harmony envelope in the piece (C).

My work with Csound is always subordinate to the musical effect, and this is why I design most of my orchestra instruments for the particular piece from the beginning. Working with a recorded material, I do my best to exploit its unique properties.

But what I like in working with Csound very much (because it is festinating and amusing) is a contribution of fortune. My best algorithms designed with Csound are result of either hard work and long searches, or simply accidental mistake.
